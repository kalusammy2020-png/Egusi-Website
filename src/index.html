OUR STORY

Born in the kitchens of
West Africa

Egusi soup traces its roots deep into Yoruba, Igbo,
and Efik culinary traditions. Ground melon seeds —
rich in fat and protein — form its golden, nutty base
Fried first in palm oil until fragrant, then simmered
with stock, leafy greens, and a chorus of spices, the
result is something between a stew and a sauce.

Served over pounded yam, eba, or fufu, it is the
meal that marks homecomings, funerals, weddings,
and ordinary Sundays alike. To eat it is to feel held
by something ancient.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Everything you've wanted to know

Egusi refers to the seeds of certain cucurbit plants — related to melon, squash, and gourd. Shelled and dried, they are ground into a powder or paste that serves as the thickening and flavouring base of the soup. They are distinct from watermelon seeds in texture and taste.

Egusi soup is almost always eaten as an accompaniment — never alone. It is paired with swallows such as pounded yam, eba (cassava flour cooked in boiling water) , amala, or fufu. You scoop the soup with small balls of the swallow, using only your right hand if following tradition.

While traditional Egusi soup often includes assorted meats, goat, stockfish, or smoked fish, a deeply satisfying vegetarian version is made using mushrooms, tofu, or extra crayfish alternatives, with vegetable stock replacing meat broth. The egusi and palm oil carry the dish beautifully on their own.

These are the two main preparation methods. Frying involves mixing ground egusi into small clumps and frying them in palm oil until golden before adding liquid — producing a chunkier, nuttier texture. Boiling dissolves the egusi into the soup for a smoother, more homogeneous consistency. Both are loved; the choice is largely regional and personal.
